## Fact
Proteins are polymers of **20 standard amino acids**, each a common backbone plus a
distinct **side chain (R group)**. Side chains group by chemistry:

| Class | Examples | Behaviour |
|---|---|---|
| Hydrophobic (nonpolar) | Ala, Val, Leu, Ile, Phe, Met, Trp | bury in the core, away from water |
| Polar uncharged | Ser, Thr, Asn, Gln, Tyr, Cys | H-bond; often at the surface |
| Positively charged (basic) | Lys, Arg, His | salt bridges, DNA/ligand binding |
| Negatively charged (acidic) | Asp, Glu | salt bridges, catalysis |
| Special | Gly (flexible), Pro (rigid kink), Cys (disulfides) | backbone geometry |

The **hydrophobic effect** — nonpolar side chains clustering to minimize
water-exposed surface — is the dominant driving force of folding.

## Why it matters
Which residues can substitute for one another (conservative vs non-conservative
mutations), why a buried hydrophobic-to-charged mutation is destabilizing, and why
coevolution signals contacts all follow from side-chain chemistry. It is the
physical basis a structure predictor implicitly learns.

## Caveats
Beyond the 20: selenocysteine/pyrrolysine (rare 21st/22nd), and pervasive
**post-translational modifications** (phosphorylation, glycosylation) change
properties but are absent from a bare sequence — so a prediction from sequence
alone omits them. Gly/Pro are backbone-geometry special cases, not simple
hydrophobic/polar members.
